Week 12 : Open Studio

This week, I focused on preparing for Open Studio which involve producing a publication, setting up A Friendly Hand interface, as well as the overall presentation of the table. It ended with a reflection on some of the comments from visitors.


Part ①: Conceptualising, designing and printing the Publication of Dreaming of an Interspecies Sensor

Over the weekend, I began reflecting on how this project started. How could the publication add value beyond documenting the process? And more importantly—who am I writing for, beyond my own desire to express?

Looking back to Week 0 of Semester One, the book What Design Can’t Do was a pivotal moment. It nudged me toward exploring computation and critical making—not just as tools, but as ways to reimagine what design could mean beyond commercial or service outcomes. Partly existential, partly driven by a search for meaning outside the typical role of a graphic designer in society.

The truth is, I still find deep comfort in arranging text and images, forming visual systems, and printing them out. So this publication is both a method and a message: a personal record of how a graphic designer attempted to connect with non-human life through speculative design. And oddly enough, through making a paper hand move—tenderly, clumsily, but with care. A book not just about outcomes—but about sensing, imagining, and relating differently.

I came up with a content flow that begins with a preface—telling the story of my doubts as a graphic designer, and the doodle I made in Week 2 that kept haunting me. The preface leads into what the book is really about: a documentation of research, experiments, conversations, and a speculative framework for other creative practioners/technologist who interested in the deep process behind 'A Friendly Hand'.

I stopped at Chapter Five—the end of Case Study One, Root Watching—because I was running out of time, and it just wasn’t possible to finish everything I had planned before Open Studios. Anyway, I printed the book on RJ paper; Excel Bulky Satin 250gsm (for the cover) and 115gsm (for the inner signatures) with Print De Creation at Sunshine Plaza. It’s saddle-stitched with the same orange thread and, for now, I’m calling it "done" enough for Open Studios.

Part ②: Setting Up Everything

It was one day before Open Studios. I reminded myself that it’s a showcase of our “work-in-progress,” took a deep breath, and continued as planned. Some small improvements included sourcing tiny pins, screws, and magnets to mount posters and images on the wall.

Another important part was designing title cards to orient each of the deliverables on the table, as well as instructional cards for the interactive artefacts.

The rest came down to personal aesthetics and instinct—trying to make the overall presentation feel organised, inviting, and a little bit pretty.

The set-up went as planned; however, the video wasn’t complete, and I didn’t feel confident playing it. So instead, I left some of the field day clips on loop. As for the rest, I thought it looked pretty neat and organised.

Part ③: Developing a Script

The other major part of my project was to be present to share my stories. I very quickly realise I left out a very important message in my set-up - which was the overall intention of why I even began dreaming of an interspecies sensor. Over time, I started developing a script to share my project. I usually start with this:

The Script-In-Workshop:

Ⓐ 'Dreaming of an Interspecies Sensor' began when I started reflecting on my personal relationship with the physical environment. What I realised was stopping me from stepping outside and “being one with nature” wasn’t a lack of desire—it was my electronic devices: my phone, my tablet, my laptop.

Ⓑ 'And then, I was curious:' How could these very tools that distance me from nature instead help me feel a sense of closeness to it? Through researching human–nature relationships, I learned that although this disconnect felt personal, it is also a societal issue—one that raises important questions about the systems we live in and the technologies we depend on.

Ⓒ I then explored interspecies communication and performance—practices that use sensors and organic matter not just to form intimacy between humans and nature, but to reimagine technology as a poetic mediator. I was fascinated by what sensors could do—not in the sense of extracting signals by clipping wires onto leaves, but in how they might still evoke connection, even in their synthetic form. That’s where the strange little doodle I made at the beginning of my project came in: a tree with multiple limbs, reaching out—almost as if waving at me. From there, the project grew into a framework guided by speculative design methodologies and the prompt: What if trees could draw, sing, and dance with us?

Ⓓ What resulted is A Friendly Hand—a strange, human-like paper hand inspired by the forest root system known as the mycorrhizal network, or the “wood-wide web.” Often visualised in science communication as hands reaching out underground, A Friendly Hand is a small sensing machine—part prosthetic, part puppet—that brings something invisible into visibility. It responds to light through a pull-and-release mechanism, showing signs of “aliveness” when it senses brightness. Because it reads raw digital values from light, different animations on the screen trigger different behaviours, making the movement of the hand feel poetic and whimsical. To me, it felt like they were dancing to light.

Week 12 : Feedback & Reflection

Not going to lie, this week was hella frustrating because I burst my Arduino and had to wait the entire week for my materials to arrive. But these are problems that couldn’t be helped without making mistakes in my first iteration. So yeah, I took this week to also work on the design of this site.

To relieve some of the frustration, I repurposed some of my old-time pixel animations that are now decking cutely out all over this site. I also visualised some mind maps from Week 3 to Week 7 to better explain my project. Overall, not a super bad week—no major feedback other than opting for a bigger dual-layer platform.
